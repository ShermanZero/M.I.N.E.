Overview

Concept

Currency as we knew it became meaningless, and value became an ever-moving bestowal placed only into unique materials which helped rapidly advance humanity. A galactic republic comprised of four major groups was formed. It became jointly known as Materials Innovation Nature Energy, simplified to MatInNaEn or M.I.N.E.. While civilians of InNaEn continued to work on Earth to maintain our beloved planet, members of Mat became intergalactic explorers, recruited to provide the materials that civilization depended on. In turn, a cycle was born, and Mat became sustained through the advancements of InNaEn.

As the founding group of M.I.N.E, technology stemmed from In. Thanks to In, the value of materials was discovered, which opened the floodgates to an explosive technology boom. After only a decade, Mat was formed to co-exist with In. Latest tech allowed explorers to quickly scout the surrounding planets, extracting planetary information. This proved invaluable, as a once hope-for-the-best approach quickly transitioned to logical and systematic.

Na came around shortly after Mat. The transition from small to large material influx sparked widespread fear of galactic resource depletion, and the harmful effects of planet scavenging. Demanding answers, citizens protested globally until Na was formed. Constant research of sustainability and environmental conservation yielded a protocol ensuring the people‚Äôs galaxy would be infinitely-sustainable. Na became responsible for the education and enforcement of this protocol.

En existed long before other groups, but burgeoned in importance after discovering what they coined as crenergy or ‚Äúcreated energy.‚Äù In reality, it wasn‚Äôt ‚Äúcreated energy‚Äù at all, but instead an extremely efficient conversion between materials and energy. Crenergy became so powerful, it exponentially surpassed even nuclear fission, and became humanity‚Äôs one and only source of energy. En became assimilated into M.I.N.E. as Mat was formed to support the near-constant intergalactic travel of explorers.

Over centuries, M.I.N.E. has continued to maintain equilibrium and while generations come and go, M.I.N.E. remains the same. The game places players into the heart of a booming economy as Material recruits. With help of the newest innovation, QriOS, an operating system ‚Äúdesigned for the curious‚Äù, players explore the infinite possibilities awaiting them at the heart of M.I.N.E.. Fueled by their own determination and promises of riches and fame, recruits set out to become the ultimate explorer.

Game Flow/Loop

While M.I.N.E. has lore (which will continue to be developed), the game itself is not a story-driven experience and should follow a basic core loop. The player starts at a HUB, selects and joins a mission, explores, gathers/trades/sells materials, and returns to the HUB. Upgrades, rewards, and leveling keep the player engaged - always striving towards becoming the wealthiest explorer in M.I.N.E..

GamePlay & Mechanics

Gameplay

Upon joining the experience, players spawn into a central ‚ÄúHUB‚Äù located deep in the vastness of space. It is the visual representation of Mat‚Äôs base of operations. The interior is decorated with futuristic technology. Life-support machines can be seen actively running to produce oxygen. Billboard-screens hanging throughout the HUB display relevant information about the G.E.M. (Galactic Exchange Market), and nearby planets available to launch to. Shops are scattered throughout that contain purchasable in-game-currency cosmetic items and upgrades. Native Robux purchasable cosmetic items can be found in specific shops as well. Upgrades are locked to in-game currency only, and players will not be offered any game-changing advantages by spending real-world money/Robux.

Every player (member of Mat) begins their experience with a Mat-issued tablet. Their tablet is their main source of interaction with global game mechanics such as joining worlds, selling/trading materials, player interaction, etc. As a unified UI, it will behave similarly to a web-based SPA (Single Page Application) where every interactable-resource they need can be found within one root-reactive UI element. For convenience, different ‚Äúpages‚Äù will be bound to controls which will bring up the specific ‚Äúpage‚Äù in the tablet without the need for the player to scroll through their tablet.

Players will be able to join planet ‚Äúmissions‚Äù which are randomly generated every few seconds. From a backend perspective, if a mission has no players, it will be discarded and its memory will be freed. A mission with at least one player will teleport all players who joined it to a private server, or from the frontend perspective, the planet. A brief arrival animation should play which allows the player to look out of an arrival pod as they descend and land on the planet. Their movement controls will be locked, and their camera will be restricted to natural head movements from a seated position. When the pod lands, its hydraulic door will open, and the player will regain control of their character, free to explore the planet.

On a planet, materials should be scattered throughout and available to gather. As a planet, it should maintain a large sense of scale unless directly specified otherwise by the game itself through UI. Players should feel a need and desire to explore, and every planet should be unique in its own way.

Players have a loosely-set amount of time to explore a planet. They must keep track of their oxygen and make sure they arrive back to their pod before their A.I.R. (Atmospheric Intake Recycler) filter becomes fully used. If they don‚Äôt make it back, they will ‚Äúfaint‚Äù and an ‚Äúextraction team‚Äù will save them at the cost of all gathered materials.

Upon a successful return to their pod, players will be prompted with the option to launch from the planet and return to the HUB with what they have gathered.

At the HUB, players can find material-grinding machines which turns materials into fine dust. This dust is the core of what‚Äôs used by En to power all operations of MatInNaEn and is the most valuable resource known to humanity. As such, in-game currency is exchanged for material dust through the G.E.M., which provides the player with opportunities to purchase upgrades.

Mechanics

  • The player‚Äôs tablet is the primary form of interaction with game systems.

  • Players must keep track of their oxygen levels when on planets to safely return to the HUB. Different planets contain different atmospheres, and as a result players‚Äô A.I.R. efficiency will vary.

  • Players can sprint at any time, but on planets this will affect their oxygen consumption.

  • Planets are pseudo-randomly generated within a defined set of rules. Different types of planets will produce different results in terrain, environment, atmosphere, and materials.

  • Planets contain randomly scattered nodes. Nodes contain materials. Players can gather these materials up to a certain amount per their backpack capacity.

  • Players can deposit collected materials into grinding machines which produce material dust. Players can sell this dust on the G.E.M. for in-game currency.

  • In-game currency can be used to purchase cosmetics or upgrades which help the player advance further and faster.

  • Players have an ‚Äúexplorer level‚Äù to stack themselves up against other players. This mechanic is conceptual and its interaction with the game will be developed alongside the game.

Options

Players should be able to bind custom controls to most, if not all, actions for PC and console. Specific game quality settings should also be available such as post-processing effects and field of view.

Setting

Environment

The HUB will be a static environment populated with sci-fi tech and miscellaneous (undetermined) objects which fit the theme of the game.

Planets, being procedurally generated, will follow constrained random rules localized to the type of planet. For example, a dry planet would have a reddish-yellowish color, little-to-no water, and potential dust-storms, whereas a wet planet would be luscious and vibrant, filled with nature, and oxygen-rich.

Interface

Visual

The majority of interactive UI should be contained within the player‚Äôs tablet. Players will be able to scroll/swipe through the different tablet ‚Äúpages‚Äù like they would do in a SPA. Some UI elements are reactive and dynamically populated by the game, such as available missions. Some default Roblox UI will be disabled, such as connected players and backpack, which will have their own stylized implementations to fit the look and feel of the game.

Other non-interactive UI elements should be limited only to display relevant information at their corresponding relevant times. For example, the player‚Äôs inventory should almost always be displayed because knowing the current state of their inventory is important to players at almost all times. These static UI elements should be condensed as much as possible to provide clear information with minimal clutter. This will be especially advantageous to mobile players, who won‚Äôt have the screen size to display tons of content.

Even still, the mobile UI will most likely require even more simplification to give the mobile users as much screen real-estate as possible.

Backend

From a web-design perspective, SPAs are taking over the internet and rightly so. Not only are they versatile, but performant as well. Filled with reactive elements, SPAs allow the client to render individual elements dynamically at their time of need. Similarly, the backend UI will closely follow this approach. Many elements will need to be dynamically generated as-needed, and each individual element should belong within a group. These groups end up displayed as tablet ‚Äúpages‚Äù which player‚Äôs can navigate through using their local and preferred controls.

Reactive/dynamic elements will be instantiated at runtime from prefabs and populate their corresponding groups.

Hybrid UI elements (such as player inventory) can have dynamic attributes, but remain unchanging at their core. For example, the player inventory only needs to display what the player currently has, versus what the player could have. To further clarify, let‚Äôs say X, Y, and Z are all possible materials. A player might have 10X and 7Y, but no Z. Omitting Z from the UI in this case allows for less clutter, and inversely, better clarity. However, X, Y, and Z are all initially loaded in memory, and only updated when needed.

Another example of a hybrid UI element is the player‚Äôs A.I.R. reading. At any given time the element itself will appear the same, but its contents (the actual numeric reading) will change. The encapsulating UI does not need to change.

Static UI elements will be used sparingly. An example of this would be the game‚Äôs build, which could also be toggled on and off. For the advanced and enthusiastic user, having a referenced build identifier provides them with basic, high-level information about the game.
